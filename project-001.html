Background

A Linguist's Question About AI

In clinical linguistics, one of the most studied phenomena is anomic aphasia — a condition in which brain injury impairs a speaker's ability to retrieve specific words. The result is a speech pattern characterized by vague, non-specific language: "the thing," "that person," "you know what I mean." Referential chains — the threads of meaning that stitch a narrative together — begin to fray. The story loses its grip on the listener.

This project began with a deceptively simple observation: large language models sometimes do something that looks remarkably similar. Not dramatically — not in a way that immediately sounds broken — but subtly. A pronoun without a clear antecedent. A reference to "the aforementioned approach" when no such approach was clearly established. A shift in topic so gradual it feels continuous, until you look back and realize the thread has been dropped entirely.

"The failure is subtle enough that a casual reader might not notice. That subtlety is precisely what makes it analytically interesting — and practically dangerous in high-stakes NLP applications."

The central question driving this project: Can frameworks developed to analyze language breakdown in aphasic patients serve as diagnostic tools for evaluating referential and coherence failures in LLM output?

Framework

From Clinical Linguistics to NLP

The study of aphasic discourse has produced several robust analytical frameworks over the past four decades. This project draws primarily on two: Referential Cohesion Analysis — which tracks how speakers introduce, maintain, and re-identify referents across a discourse — and Rhetorical Structure Theory (RST), which models how discourse segments relate to one another through coherence relations such as elaboration, contrast, cause, and concession.

In aphasic speech research, breakdowns in referential cohesion are well-documented across aphasia subtypes. Anomic aphasia in particular is associated with empty anaphora (use of pronouns or deictic expressions without established referents), lexical substitution errors (substituting a specific term with a semantically vague superordinate — "the animal" instead of "the dog"), and topic drift without the discourse markers that typically signal intentional shifts.

These three failure types — empty anaphora, vague lexical substitution, and unmarked topic drift — form the core of the Aphasic Coherence Taxonomy (ACT) adapted in this project as a diagnostic lens for LLM output.

Failure Type In Aphasic Speech In LLM Output
Empty Anaphora Pronoun used without established referent; listener cannot resolve "it" or "they" Model uses "this approach" or "the method" without having named a specific method in context
Vague Lexical Substitution Specific noun replaced by vague superordinate ("the thing," "that person") Model substitutes precise terminology with hedged, generic phrasing mid-explanation
Unmarked Topic Drift Speaker shifts topic without discourse markers; narrative loses coherence Model shifts focus across a long generation without signalling the transition, producing text that feels thematically connected but isn't

Findings

Key Findings

Across 40 GPT-4 generation samples (long-form explanatory tasks, narrative completions, and multi-turn dialogue), the ACT framework identified coherence failures in 78% of outputs exceeding 600 tokens. Shorter outputs showed significantly fewer failures, suggesting that coherence degradation is strongly correlated with generation length — a finding consistent with what clinical linguists observe in extended aphasic monologue tasks.

Implications

Why This Matters for NLP

The significance of this work lies not just in identifying that LLMs fail at referential coherence — that much has been noted in passing by others — but in providing a linguistically grounded, reproducible taxonomy for classifying and communicating those failures. Clinical linguists have spent decades refining these tools. Importing them into NLP evaluation creates several immediate practical opportunities:

  • 01 The ACT framework can be operationalized as an automated evaluation metric, enabling large-scale benchmarking of LLM coherence that goes beyond surface-level fluency scores like BLEU or ROUGE.
  • 02 Identifying the failure types allows targeted fine-tuning: training data can be curated to specifically address vague lexical substitution and empty anaphora, rather than treating coherence as a monolithic quality.
  • 03 In high-stakes domains — medical documentation, legal drafting, educational content — referential failures of this kind carry real risk. A framework for detecting them before deployment is a meaningful safety contribution.
  • 04 The parallel between aphasic and AI discourse failure opens a genuinely new research direction: using language pathology as a mirror for AI language pathology, with implications for both fields.

Conclusion

This project demonstrates that the tools of clinical linguistics are not just historically interesting — they are practically useful for the evaluation of modern AI language systems. The same frameworks developed to understand how a stroke patient loses the thread of a story can be applied, with meaningful results, to understanding how a billion-parameter language model does the same thing.

The finding that coherence failures are perceptually subtle but structurally significant is particularly consequential. It means that existing human evaluation methods are likely undercounting these failures — and that a linguistically-informed framework is not a luxury, but a necessity.

Future work will extend the taxonomy to cover additional aphasia subtypes — including Broca's and Wernicke's — and explore whether distinct LLM architectures produce distinct failure signatures, in the same way that different brain lesion sites produce distinct aphasic profiles.
