About

I am a Computational Linguist with a graduate background from the University at Buffalo, specializing in semantics, discourse, and narrative coherence. My work sits at the intersection of how human language breaks down — and how artificial language systems mirror those same fractures. I bring an artist's eye for pattern and an analyst's precision for structure to every problem I work on.

Projects

Project 001 — Featured

When AI Loses the Thread: Discourse Coherence Failure in LLMs

Drawing on frameworks developed in clinical linguistics to study aphasic narrative breakdown, this project applies the same analytical lens to large language models. Using coherence relation analysis and referential chain tracking, I identify and taxonomize the specific ways LLMs lose narrative thread in extended generation tasks — and map these failure modes onto established aphasia subtypes.

Project 002

Referential Chains in Aphasic Narrative: A Corpus Study

A structured annotation and analysis of referential chain integrity across aphasic and non-aphasic speech samples. Documents how different aphasia subtypes affect the ability to maintain coherent reference — with implications for evaluating similar breakdowns in generative AI systems.

Project 003

Scalar Implicature Stress-Testing in GPT-4

A systematic evaluation of how GPT-4 handles scalar implicature — the pragmatic inferences triggered by words like "some," "possible," and "warm." Documents consistent failure patterns, categorizes them by implicature type, and proposes a diagnostic framework for future LLM benchmarking.
